Emotional Eating

Disconnected eating is when we eat but we’re not hungry at prescribed meal times or we continue to eat even when we feel full. It is often associated with emotions such as rejection, anxiety, and fear. Studies have shown that young girls who feel anxiety often mistake this feeling for hunger. In addition, disconnected eating often results from dieting. When a person is starving, it’s difficult to control binges when food becomes available. If a person consistently eats beyond a feeling of fullness, weight gain becomes an issue. “Emotional” eating is when food is used as a comfort or a distraction from emotions: “Eating in response to emotions interferes with the body’s natural physical cues of hunger, fullness, appetite, and satiety” (Kratina, King, and Hayes, 1999).

Regular dieting seems to increase disconnected or emotional eating considerably. The following problems appear when an individual is always on a diet (Kratina, King, and Hayes, 1999):

  • “Preoccupation with food,
  • hunger intolerance,
  • overeating or binge eating,
  • eating in response to emotions,
  • weight gain… the absolute opposite of the results the individual wanted.”
    • (Kratina et al, 1999)
